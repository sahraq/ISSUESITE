Statistics

Somalia has one of the highest rates of Female Genital Mutilation (FGM) in the world, with 98% of women and girls aged 15-49 having undergone the procedure. Most of these procedures are performed between the ages of 5 and 11. The most common type practiced is Type III (infibulation), which is the most severe form of cutting.

Health Risks Associated with FGM

The practice poses significant health risks, contributing to increased maternal and newborn mortality rates. Women who have undergone FGM are at a greater risk of suffering complications during childbirth, including hemorrhaging and obstructed labor, leading to higher maternal mortality rates. In fact, FGM increases the risk of childbirth complications by 50% and newborn deaths by up to 55%.

Long-Term Health Consequences

FGM is also linked to a range of chronic health issues, such as urinary infections, chronic pain, cysts, and infertility. Studies indicate that approximately 30% of women who have undergone FGM experience complications that require medical attention later in life.
